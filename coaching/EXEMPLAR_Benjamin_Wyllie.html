1

The one-line verdict

Why it didn't flip

Right instinct, no ask.

What actually happened

Brandon built genuine understanding, asked the perfect question — are you guys more proactive or reactive? — then, when the customer leaned in to buy, re-explained the value of the specialist visit and let her sign a "we'll wait" form.

What flips it

The moment she said she wanted to start saving, the sale was warm. The FSG move is to stop selling the idea and book the no-cost specialist visit to a real day and time — convert the signal into a calendar slot before another word about value.

4

Call timeline — how the flip slipped away

Phase 1 · Outdoor check-in

Nails the teaching

Walks her through the compressor amp-draw vs. the manufacturer max — this at the moment is pulling 118… what's the max rating for this part. So it's 112. Heart analogy, videos of the wobbling fan motor. She's listening but only answers Yeah.

Phase 2 · First wait signal

An objection surfaces early — and isn't worked

She reveals the prior tech (Jose) already flagged it, and says I know it's already old, eventually it's gonna fail, but we'll just wait for it. This is the objection to handle. Brandon acknowledges and moves on to finish the inspection.

Phase 3 · Attic + rapport

Warm, human, a little long

Checks the UV, filter, blower; friendly card-game chat. Good rapport, but the wait-objection is now sitting unaddressed in the background.

Phase 4 · The full draw-out

Be A Nerd — Strong

Draws the whole system, indoor and outdoor, thermostat to coil to vents. This is his best moment: This cooling coil is like a radiator, but instead of getting hot, it gets cold.

Phase 5 · Presentation

Clean options, priced plainly

Two forms, immediate vs. future, plus the specialist option, framed around avoiding a mid-summer surprise. Say-It is solid. Then she asks how much is it for a brand new AC unit? — genuine curiosity — and he correctly routes exact pricing to the specialist.

Phase 6 · Asks permission to fail

The perfect question, then a surrender

He asks the gold line — are you guys more proactive or reactive? — but when she says we can wait, he agrees and asks the surprise is fine with you guys?, then reaches for signatures on the wait form.

Phase 7 · The buying signal

She hands him the sale — he explains instead

Customer: I wish I have idea so we, we can start saving because I know I heard it's really like that. Brandon: That's what we give you guys, this option, then drifts into a pricing-history story. No day booked, no appointment made.

Phase 8 · Close

Signs the wait form; no review ask

She signs the acknowledgement, they chat about houses, he leaves. The flip that was live 60 seconds earlier is gone, and the visit ends with no five-star ask.

5

The decisive moment, word for word

Phase 7 · the buying signal

"I wish I had an idea so we can start saving."

What he said

That's what we give you guys, this option. So the — have you guys had a visit before? …then a story about pricing changing three times.

What it was

Not an objection — an invitation. She's telling him she wants the number so she can budget for it. That's a yes to the specialist visit waiting on a day.

Book it — the word-for-word fix

"Perfect — that's exactly what our comfort specialist is for. No cost, no commitment, he just gets you the real number so you can start planning. He's out this way Thursday morning — does that work, or is afternoon better?"

Then stop talking. Let her pick a time. The goal isn't to convince her the visit is valuable — she just told you it is. The goal is a slot on the calendar before you say another word.

Weak

Every explanation ended in "Yeah" — she never diagnosed it

PatternBrandon explains → Customer: "Yeah." → next topic. When he asked "did Josué ever go over this?" the answer was "No" — a wide-open door to let her conclude it herself.

The fix After you draw the picture, hand her the diagnosis and wait: "So with that compressor already past its limit — what do you think happens on the first 110-degree day?" When the customer says "it'll break," the urgency is hers, not yours — and the wait objection often dissolves on its own.

What he did right — real strength, build on it

  • Elite teaching (Be A Nerd — Strong). Heart/compressor, radiator, blow-dryer, flashlight battery, paperclip fatigue, plus live videos and before/after photos — you could eat your dinner off of here.
  • Clear, honest options (Say It — Solid). Named every price without flinching and separated immediate from future per system.
  • The right closing instinct. are you guys more proactive or reactive? is exactly the reframe question — he just didn't hold the line after asking it.
  • Genuine, easy rapport. Warm with the family throughout; the trust needed to close was clearly there.
8

This week's drill — the signal-to-slot reflex

Practice until it's automatic

Trigger: the customer says anything like "we want to be ready" / "start saving" / "know the cost."

Response, within 10 seconds:

"Love that — let's get you that number with zero commitment. I've got our specialist out this way [day] — morning or afternoon?"

Rehearse it out loud with a manager five times before the next maintenance day. One reflex — signal → booked slot — is the whole difference between this call and a flip.

Bottom line

Brandon doesn't need to be more convincing — he needs to make the ask. He builds more trust and understanding than almost anyone on the board, then talks past the exact moment the customer leans in. Drill one move: when you hear the signal, stop selling and book the appointment. That's it. That's the flip.
